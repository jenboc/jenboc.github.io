## My Experience 

Last Saturday, me and 2 other friends took part in a Competitive Programming Competition ([The UKIEPC](http://ukiepc.info/) if you're interested). Now, technically I do have 10 years of programming experience. However, very little of this is the "competitive" sort. If you don't know what Competitive Programming is, it essentially involves programmers (usually in groups) solving logic/programming questions in a short time frame - for the UKIEPC we had 5 hours for 14 problems. If this sounds easy to you, go ahead, find a UKIEPC problem set and give it a try but I don't think my decade of programming experience nor my fair share of codewars was enough to prepare me for some of the problems I faced. 

Before the competition started, I didn't think that the team would perform well and having seen the sort of problems that were asked in previous years I thought that we would get 1 or 2 problems correct maximum. In reality, we answered 5 correctly and placed top 100 nationwide and top 10 in our university. We could've possibly placed higher, but a few of our solutions were too slow and didn't satisfy the time constraints for the problem. Maybe a language change would've helped, since we were using python (notoriously slow!)

I must say, it was a lot of fun. I didn't really know this sort of competition existed before applying to take part, so this was my first ever experience with it. For me, the most fun aspect of programming _is_ the problem solving, which is exactly what competitive programming is about.

## Looking to the Future

I would love to do this competition again, maybe give the Oxbridge & Imperial teams a run for their money but to do this I would need a lot more practice. I would also like to use these competitions as a way to improve my C/C++ as I would like to use these languages in my side projects as well and I believe that the simplest way to learn a language well is to write, and read, a lot of code in that language and these problems are almost perfect for doing this.
